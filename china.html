China

My experience in China

China has always held a special allure for me — a country where ancient temples stand proudly beside gleaming skyscrapers, and where the rhythm of modern life beats in harmony with centuries-old traditions. When I finally embarked on my long-awaited trip, I wasn’t just looking for places to see; I was seeking moments that would make me feel connected to something timeless. Beijing was my first chapter. The air was crisp, filled with the aroma of roasted chestnuts and the faint echo of bicycle bells weaving through narrow hutongs. The Forbidden City stretched endlessly before me, a labyrinth of golden roofs and crimson gates whispering stories of emperors and dynasties. As I wandered through its grand courtyards, I imagined the footsteps of those who once ruled this vast nation. Later, I climbed the gentle slope of Jingshan Park, where a panoramic view of the palace unfolded beneath the soft glow of the sunset. From above, Beijing felt eternal — a living museum that still hums with everyday life.

No journey to Beijing would be complete without walking the Great Wall of China. I chose the Mutianyu section, known for its quiet beauty and sweeping mountain views. The climb was steep, and my legs burned, but reaching the top filled me with awe. The wall wound endlessly over the hills, like a dragon guarding the heart of China. The wind carried silence and strength — a reminder of the millions of hands that built this wonder stone by stone. From the echoes of the past, I sped into the future aboard a high-speed train to Shanghai. The city’s skyline rose like a dream — glittering towers reflecting on the shimmering Huangpu River. I strolled along The Bund, where colonial architecture from another era faced the neon-lit marvels of Pudong. At night, the city felt alive in every direction, as if the stars had descended to earth. Yet, amid the glass and steel, I found calm in the Yu Garden, where ponds of koi fish rippled beneath curved bridges and ancient pavilions. Sitting there with a cup of fragrant jasmine tea, I realized that Shanghai’s true beauty lies in its balance — the dance between stillness and motion, history and progress. A few days later, I found myself in Xi’an, one of China’s oldest and most storied cities. The Terracotta Army left me speechless — thousands of life-sized warriors standing silently in their earthen chambers, each face distinct, each detail carved with devotion. It was as if time itself had paused to preserve this tribute to eternity. Later, I rode a bicycle along the ancient Xi’an City Wall, feeling the wind rush past as the city buzzed below. As night fell, I wandered through the Muslim Quarter, where sizzling street food filled the air with spice and smoke. I tried roujiamo, the famous “Chinese hamburger,” its savory filling wrapped in warm bread — a small masterpiece of flavor that somehow captured the city’s spirit. My final destination was Guilin, a place that looked like it had leapt out of a painting. The Li River flowed like silk between emerald-green karst mountains, their reflections shimmering on the still water. I floated downstream on a bamboo raft, watching fishermen with cormorant birds glide gracefully nearby. The world was hushed, except for the gentle rhythm of the paddle and the soft rustle of the wind. Later, in Yangshuo, I cycled through the countryside, passing rice fields, orange groves, and villages where time seemed to slow. As the sun dipped behind the mountains, the sky turned gold, and I felt an overwhelming peace that words could barely describe. As my plane took off from China, I looked down at the patchwork of cities and rivers and realized that this journey had given me far more than memories. It had taught me to see beauty in contrasts — in the old and the new, the busy and the still, the known and the mysterious. China is a country that doesn’t just show you its stories; it invites you to live them, to listen, and to carry a piece of them wherever you go. I left with my heart full, my camera overflowing, and the quiet promise that one day, I would return — because some stories are too grand to be told in just one trip.
